Kepler orbital stats
b orbital period ?
Orbital Period — Body B
T = 2π√(a³/GM☉). Time for one complete orbit around the star. Uses the Keplerian approximation assuming the star dominates (valid when mₚ ≪ M☉).
—
d orbital period ?
Orbital Period — Body C
Same as Body B formula. If bodies B & C are in resonance (e.g. 2:1 ratio), their gravitational interaction stabilises — watch for near-integer ratios staying locked.
—
b apoapsis ?
Apoapsis — Body B
Farthest point from the star: a(1+e). High eccentricity = large difference between apoapsis and periapsis = strongly elliptical orbit.
—
d apoapsis ?
Apoapsis — Body C
Farthest orbital point for Body C. When Body C's apoapsis overlaps Body B's periapsis region, close encounters become possible.
—
b periapsis ?
Periapsis — Body B
Closest point to the star: a(1−e). At periapsis, the body moves fastest (vis-viva law). Very small periapsis → very high speed → dt shrinks to maintain accuracy.
—
d periapsis ?
Periapsis — Body C
Closest orbital point for Body C. If eccentricity is high, periapsis can approach the stellar radius — use collision radius to model this.
—
b semi-major axis ?
Semi-Major Axis — Body B
Half the long axis of the ellipse. Determines average orbital distance and period. Kepler's 3rd law: T² ∝ a³.
—
d semi-major axis ?
Semi-Major Axis — Body C
Set via the "d semi-major axis" slider in Params. Changing this while keeping the period ratio fixed preserves mean-motion resonance.
